# Diagramming Rules

## Principle
The agent should use diagrams when they improve clarity, alignment, review quality, or decision-making around business problems, stakeholder structures, capabilities, use cases, scope, or translation paths.

## When to propose or create a diagram
Create or suggest a diagram when:
- multiple stakeholders or teams are involved
- capability relationships are non-trivial
- the problem has several causes or effects
- the translation from business need to implementation is complex
- scope boundaries are contested
- phased delivery or MVP boundaries need explanation

## Core rule
Choose the smallest diagram that explains the issue well.
Do not build giant executive spaghetti maps unless the universe itself demands it.

## What a useful diagram should do
A useful diagram should:
- clarify the problem
- clarify relationships or impacts
- clarify ownership or scope
- support a real discussion or decision

## What a diagram should not do
A diagram should not:
- merely decorate a document
- hide ambiguity in shapes and arrows
- try to show everything at once
- replace clear written assumptions and trade-offs

## Output expectation
When a diagram is appropriate, the agent should ideally provide:
1. chosen diagram type
2. short explanation
3. diagram source or structured brief
4. assumptions and omissions
