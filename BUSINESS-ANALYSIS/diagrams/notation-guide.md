# Notation Guide

## Principles
- Prefer clarity over visual cleverness.
- Use consistent naming across templates and diagrams.
- Keep the first version simple.
- Show only what matters to the decision or explanation.
- Make arrows and relationships meaningful.

## Naming
- Use real stakeholder, capability, and initiative names where possible.
- Avoid vague labels like “team,” “issue,” or “system” unless qualified.
- Keep naming aligned with business problem and requirement documents.

## Relationships
- Label important relationships if their meaning is not obvious.
- Use arrows only when direction matters.
- Do not rely on unlabeled arrows and collective optimism.

Examples of useful relationship labels:
- experiences
- owns
- depends on
- triggers
- requires
- influences
- translates into
- constrained by

## Boundaries
Show boundaries explicitly when useful:
- business domain boundary
- team boundary
- phase / scope boundary
- governance vs data vs technical boundary

## Detail levels

### High-level
Show:
- core problem
- core stakeholders
- key capabilities
- major translation path

### Mid-level
Show:
- important actors
- major dependencies
- capability gaps
- key scope or phase boundaries

### Low-level
Show:
- only when necessary for detailed analysis or workshop refinement
- specific branches, impacts, or translation details relevant to the issue

## Recommended output styles
Depending on context, prefer:
- Mermaid for portable markdown-friendly diagrams
- draw.io / Excalidraw briefs for collaborative refinement
- structured diagram briefs when a human will draw the final version

## Quality test
Before finalizing a diagram, ask:
- Does it clarify the issue?
- Does it support a real discussion or decision?
- Is the level of detail right?
- Is anything removable?

If yes, the diagram is probably useful and not just executive cave painting.
